Leveraged Acquisition and their Tax Impacts

 

The concept of leverage transaction is quite interesting in itself. The leveraged transaction generally happens when a smaller companies seeks to acquire a chunk or whole of the larger entity. Also known as leveraged buy outs (LBO) is a situation when the fund is acquired by raising fund through loans or any other option available to the buyer.

 

The LBO gives some major problems with reference to taxation under the Income Tax Act, the provisions referred is from the old Income Tax Act of 1961. Section 14A provides that any expenditure made to earn an income which is exempt from taxation, such expenditure shall be liable to be disallowed. Therefore, any interest expenditure which is accrued to the buyer company shall be disallowed according to rule 8D of Income Tax Rules, if the debt is used to buy shares and the earnings at the hands of the buyer company is in the form of dividends which is an exempt income.

 

This tax issue becomes of utmost relevance because of a simple reason that to the acquirer the disallowed interest component is a dead loss. This becomes an important issue for consideration because the tax component is pivotal in deciding whether to go for ‘business acquisition’ or ‘share acquisition’.

 

Further there is another option available to duck this tax disallowance and that is going for a fold-up. Folding up a acquirer entity and reverse merging it into the acquired entity is another gate-away when it comes to the tax benefit, as this particular transaction now will be considered as a transaction in the furtherance of business and the expenditure is now allowable under section 36(iii) of Income Tax Act.
